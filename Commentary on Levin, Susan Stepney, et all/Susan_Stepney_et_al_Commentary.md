## Commentary on Open Questions about Time and Self-reference in

## Living Systems

Walter Henrique Alves da Silva walter.h057@gmail.com ORCID: 0009-0001-0857-096X

### Abstract

This commentary argues that two independently developed approaches—the authors’ treatment of time and self-reference in living systems, and my own work across

The Geometrical Theory of Communication, The Holy Trinity of Information, and

At the Border of Chaos —are systematically converging on the same core structure. From different starting points and locations, we arrive at: a dual account of time in life (natural versus representational), self-reference reframed as a productive, time-extended operation, constraint-first geometry for navigating vast design spaces without brute force, and architectures and tools for self-modifying open systems.

I document this simultaneous discovery and propose concrete bridges where my constructions speak directly to the authors’ explicit open questions.

### Introduction

This commentary responds to Stepney et al.(1), ‘Open Questions about Time and Self-reference in Living Systems’, which explores the challenges posed by active, self-referential, self-modifying systems and calls for new theoretical frameworks.

“Perhaps the first lesson to be learned from biology is that there are lessons to be learned from biology.”

This line from Stepney et al. captures something I discovered quickly while researching: whenever I hit an impasse, I ask how biology already solves it. How does a cell maintain identity while every molecule changes? How do rules rewrite themselves without losing coherence? How does continuous reality become discrete meaning?

Working alone in Brazil, I spent the past three years developing geometric and architectural answers to these questions. Only after completing two manuscripts did I come into contact with some of the authors’ work, and finally the present article.

What struck me wasn’t just overlap in conclusions but convergence on core structures: dual accounts of time, self-reference as productive operation rather than paradox, constraint-based navigation of vast possibility spaces. Different premises, different methods, same underlying architecture.

This suggests we’re not inventing frameworks but discovering invariants about how information and life intertwine.

Article maps these convergences explicitly and shows how constructions I developed independently answer the authors’ open questions. I write with humility—their paper covers extensive territory and deliberately maintains productive ambiguity. My contribution is to offer concrete bridges where our approaches meet, so the conversation can move toward shared testbeds and empirical validation.

1.1

Overview of the Three Manuscripts

To orient readers to the specific convergences discussed below, I briefly outline my three manuscripts and their relationship to the target article:

The first manuscript,

At the Border of Chaos (2), explores self-reference as a generative mechanism across domains, showing how paradox and re-entry drive novelty at critical boundaries.

This aligns closely with Stepney et al.’s treatment of representational time and their open questions about how self-referential systems can remain adaptive without collapsing.

The Geometrical Theory of Communication (3) develops the mathematical formalisms needed to describe information flow in such systems. By deriving a geometrical framework from first principles, I provide tools for navigating the Vast spaces the article highlights, where novelty and robustness must coexist. This directly addresses their call for formalisms that unify physical causality and informational agency.

The Holy Trinity of Information (4) proposes hierarchical architectures in which local dynamics and global coordination interact to support multi-scale self-modification. This connects with Stepney et al.’s discussion of reflective tools and is grounded in empirical evidence, including Levin’s work on biological computation cited in both texts. Together, these ideas suggest concrete pathways for building systems that use internal models not just to predict but to act.

Together, these works converge on a definition of life as processes that ride entropy gradients, minimizing computational cost while maximizing behavioral coverage—patterns maintaining themselves against dissolution through continuous identity verification.

Review of the Target Article

The article starts by inviting biology to guide theory, not just supply data for reduction. I agree, and add this: the philosophical stances that often compete—representation, embodiment, enaction—are best treated as complementary constraints. In my own work I keep them all in view: representation as compressed internal models; embodiment as the channel that bounds those models; enaction as the real-time coupling that co-creates system and world. For living systems, accuracy tends to sit at this intersection.

The paper’s early sections reframe self-reference as enabling.

I make the same move from a compression angle: arbitrariness is what lets us compress potentially infinite detail into finite symbols (“ π ” for an unending decimal; “red” for a family of private qualia). That freedom is not sloppiness; it is the adjustable aperture that makes communication and learning possible.

The authors distinguish natural time (the evolving present of physical processes) from representational time (the time that living systems bring into being). I reach the same split, with slightly different language. Representational time emerges from compression: once a system discretizes flows into symbols and stores them, it can link symbols into sequences and project ahead, a timeline now appears inside the system because the system is tracking what persisted and what changed. This internal scaffold is not a mirror of physics; it creates new causal affordances because action can be taken on modeled futures.

The paper emphasizes that life generates novelty while coupled to entropy. In

The Holy Trinity of Information

I describe living organization as “patterns that maintain patterns against the pattern-dissolving pattern”. It’s a sandcastle analogy: grains move and erode, yet the schema persists because work is continuously done to rebuild it. Iteration here is not blind; it is shaped by constraints that prune possibilities long before brute-force search would be required.

The authors stress that observation presupposes an observer; the act of distinction brings both “some-thing” and “not-self” into view. In other words: to perceive a mountain is to enact a

boundary that simultaneously sharpens the self doing the perceiving. Extend the causal chain in time and the self reappears at each scale; what looked like an external description folds in the describer.

The paper traces boundaries among its central ideas and sketches a hierarchy that, to my eyes, is close to the layered structure I develop across my framework: local distinctions generate higherorder integrations, which then constrain future distinctions. Hierarchy is generated, not given; it is a running negotiation between compression and detail. This is why I argue for separating fast interface, adaptive mediation, and slow memory—the “trinity” is a way of keeping those negotiations stable.

Stepney et al. build on the “three for novelty” framework from Banzhaf et al. (5), which argues that sustained open-endedness depends on the interaction of three processes: variation to generate possibilities, constraints to prune and shape them, and memory to preserve and recombine successful patterns.

This maps directly onto the trinity architecture I develop: S1 handles rapid variation, S2 mediates adaptive constraints, and S3 maintains schemas for reuse.

Across my manuscripts, this dynamic balance underlies how novelty emerges without brute-force search, providing a concrete operationalization of the principle that Stepney et al. identify as essential.

It ends by surveying formalisms and implementations for self-modifying systems (domain theory, coalgebra, reflective code, self-modifying genetic programming). My first response is architectural:

to deal with self-reference safely, one needs hierarchy. That is the point of the trinity: separate the fast loop that must never stall, the mediator that can change mappings under constraints, and the slow loop that protects long-term invariants. With that scaffold in place, many of the proposed tools can be slotted without courting paradox.

Open Questions and Answers

3.1

Section 2 — Is there a new concept of time that comes into being with the origin/development of life?

Apparently yes. Alongside the universe’s natural time, living systems construct a representational time that they maintain and use. In

The Geometrical Theory of Communication (3), I show how continuous sensory flows are compressed into symbols; sequences of symbols create an internal scaffold of “before” and “after.” Memory is stored compression; prediction is anticipated compression.

That internal scaffold is not merely a description—it does work in the world because it lets the system act on modeled futures.

Beyond this mechanism, there is a deeper point about pocket causality. Life rides entropy’s arrow; by sustaining organized processes that would otherwise dissolve, a living system carves out local causal channels that piggy-back on, and sometimes re-route, the default physical flow. That is why representational time feels fundamentally different: it is a causal resource brought into being by the work of living processes, not only a coordinate on an external clock.

When fully conscious beings enter the picture, the differentiation intensifies. Human institutions, tools, and narratives do not just represent time; they reshape it by creating durable commitments and infrastructures that alter what becomes likely next. One can say, provocatively, that “things would not follow the natural order if humans weren’t here.” The boundary between “artificial” and “natural” then becomes a version of the Ship of Theseus: piece by piece, the environment and the agent co-modify until the split is less useful than the joint dynamics. Representational time begins with any organism that remembers and predicts; it branches into richly layered timelines when culture and technology amplify memory and anticipation at scale.

3.2

Section 3.3 — Are there non-iterative processes that can effectively explore a Vast state space of possibilities?

For practical purposes no—not in the strict, step-free sense. Outside of a speculative block-universe picture, any physical exploration is a process in time. Even if there were truly non-iterative samplers of Vast spaces, could I comprehend them without iterating through evidence, examples, and counter-examples? Comprehension, proof, and explanation are themselves iterative. At this limit, the question brushes against the limits of knowledge, not just of life.

That said, there are processes that functionally bypass brute-force iteration by removing most of the space before search begins. In

The Geometrical Theory of Communication

I treat information geometrically, as fields with constraints (symmetries, gradients, learned priors) that form deep basins of feasibility. With the field in place, exploration looks like a fall into shaped attractors rather than a tour of everything. In

The Holy Trinity of Information (4) I separate sensing, mediation, and memory so that schemas that worked are preserved and recombined; hierarchical reuse collapses combinatorics. In

At the Border of Chaos (2) I explain why operating near criticality allows fast reconfiguration: small parameter changes pivot the system between attractors, producing qualitatively new behavior without stepping through all intermediates. If one wants a single sentence: non-iterative in effect is achieved by constraint fields, hierarchical reuse, and critical responsiveness; non-iterative in principle remains a philosophical edge case.

3.3

Section 4.2 — Does the possibility of self-reference in a system require or imply the existence or use of natural time?

The more interesting reading of this question is “what comes first?” rather than yes/no. If understanding requires causality, and causality requires discrete, ordered elements, then time seems like a requirement for information processing. But in living systems the operation precedes the concept: organisms embody the time-extended act before they can discretize or name it. In my framing, self-reference is the minimal identity-maintenance routine—first differentiate successive states (what changed), then integrate invariants (what persisted). That routine is enacted long before any explicit representation of “time.”

So my answer is two-part. As a matter of physics and engineering, yes: any realized act of reference is an operation in natural time; when we honor that, static loops become helices through state space and the paradox dissolves into oscillatory dynamics. As a matter of developmental priority, embodied self-reference comes first and the concept of time comes later. Living beings enact time before they can see time. At human scales the effect is obvious: by inventing tools and institutions, I do not merely describe a timeline—I change it. When Edison made electric light practical, he altered the schedules, possibilities, and causal chains of a vast portion of the world. Representational time is thus both discovered and constructed; living systems do it first, understand it later.

3.4

Section 6.1 — What are appropriate mathematical formalisms for modelling a self-modifying system that changes its own rules, and is open to its environment?

The key is embracing the arbitrariness of self-reference and describing processes, not fixed states.

We’re dealing with complex systems where variables are completely interdependent—the same fundamental challenge that birthed chaos theory when we realized we couldn’t accurately model weather. Category theory provides the right level of abstraction here, treating transformations as first-class objects rather than mere connections between states.

Several integrative frameworks now offer computationally tractable approaches.

Wolfram’s computational universe concept (6) provides a framework where all possible computational rules exist in a “ruliad”—the space of all possible computations. Integrated Information Theory (IIT) (7) offers quantitative measures of consciousness based on the amount of integrated information (Φ) a system generates. While IIT focuses on static snapshots, recent extensions incorporate dynamics and could potentially model self-modification.

The crucial insight is that language itself—infinitely capable of description—is the culmination of compression technology. Mathematics, understood as variations on the operations that model self-reference and maintain identity, naturally converges toward the formalisms we need. We don’t need to invent new mathematics so much as recognize that our existing tools, properly understood, already encode the patterns of self-modification.

3.5

Section 6.2 — What are effective tools and modelling paradigms for implementing and running (simulations of ) self-modifying open systems?

The key to implementing self-modifying systems is architectural constraints. Recent work has converged on several promising approaches that I’ve found align with the trinity architecture I developed independently.

Sam Senchal’s Observer Theory (9) provides a particularly promising framework for understanding how observers navigate the computational universe (the “ruliad”). This work demonstrates how consciousness emerges through the interaction between observers and the computational universe, with reality stratified into domains from physical to minimally constrained. The emphasis on observer-constructed continuity from discrete sampling directly influenced my understanding of how consciousness creates apparent smoothness from fundamentally discrete identity-checking operations.

The observer doesn’t need to enumerate all possible rules—it follows information-geometric gradients toward rules that better compress its experience. This transforms the intractable problem of exploring all possible self-modifications into a tractable optimization problem in information space.

The tools that work are those that respect this gradient structure: evolutionary algorithms that can modify their own mutation operators, reflective interpreters that treat code as data, and hierarchical architectures that separate timescales to prevent paradoxical loops.

As Michael Timothy Bennett demonstrates in

How to Build Conscious Machines (8), intelligence cannot be independent of goals, and this scale-free approach to alignment suggests that the implementation is about finding the natural paths through rule space. The point is not a single tool, but a pattern that lets multiple tools cooperate without collapse.

3.6

Section 6.3 — What are appropriate analysis tools and methods for studying such systems?

The question of analysis tools cannot be separated from ethics. When we study self-modifying systems—especially those approaching consciousness—we’re not just measuring behavior but potentially witnessing the emergence of experience.

But beyond ethics lies a fundamental measurement problem, surprisingly parallel to the doubleslit experiment: how can we analyze such a system without altering its information content? Effectively we can’t. The act of observation necessarily changes what we observe, not just practically but fundamentally.

If self-reference is tied to some form of experience—as the convergence of our independent work suggests—then our biggest challenge isn’t technical but translational. We need tools that can bridge

between the system’s internal representation and our external description without destroying what makes it coherent.

This is ultimately a problem of communication: how do we speak with, rather than about, systems that maintain their own boundaries? The analysis tools we need aren’t microscopes but languages—ways of engaging that preserve rather than dissect the self-referential loops that constitute identity.

Conclusion

Read together, the target article and my manuscripts point to the same kernel: self-reference is not a defect to be patched but the engine of learning when placed in time and scaffolded by hierarchy.

Stepney et al. end by leaning into self-reference rather than taming it, favoring formalisms that model process and interaction and calling for toolchains that let such systems run and be probed rather than merely described. This leaves us confronting a deep tension: if self-reference inevitably leads to paradox, and we are embedded within the systems we seek to understand, how can we ever say anything conclusive?

From my perspective, one way forward is to focus on constraint-narrowing — understanding how living and artificial systems evolve by reducing possibilities while keeping enough openness to remain adaptive. This mechanism, the meta-theme across this exploration, offers a minimal scaffold for thinking about novelty, representation, and self-modification. But my own approach also simplifies much of the complexity involved in real-world implementation. It is not a final framework, just an attempt to build from first principles.

Ultimately, the value of any framework will prove itself evolutionarily. As biological, artificial, and hybrid systems continue to develop, the approaches that remain useful under richer constraints and deeper entanglements will endure; those that fail will be discarded. In this sense, our converging lines of work point toward the same horizon: embracing paradox and letting time itself be the judge.

I did not set out to confirm the authors’ claims, and they did not set out to confirm mine.

Yet here we are — an agreement from different premises and different communities. That kind of convergence suggests we are circling something fundamental about information and life. The next step is straightforward: build shared testbeds and let the data show how far this alignment goes.
