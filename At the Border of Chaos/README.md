## Summary

When you name something, you draw a boundary in conceptual space. That act — discretization — is a geometric operation, and every limit that appears across language, mathematics, and science traces back to the geometry of that operation rather than to any flaw in the particular theory that ran into it.

The paper makes this case by walking the same boundary operation across domains. Theseus's ship, Achilles and the tortoise, Russell's set, Gödel's incompleteness, wave-particle duality: each is a system complete enough to describe its own domain encountering the edge of what discretization can contain. The paradoxes are not anomalies; they are the structure of the operation becoming visible.

From that ground, the paper argues that observer-dependent projection produces complementary rather than contradictory descriptions, that entropy and causality emerge as dual outcomes of the same discretization act, and that the limits of formal systems are structural consequences of geometric closure rather than accidents of incomplete formalization.

## Role in the Series

The boundary operation identified here is the primitive that every later paper formalizes. The Geometrical Theory of Communication works out what it computes; the Zeroth Law derives the minimum algebra that can run it correctly; the Holy Trinity of Information builds the architecture that keeps it stable across timescales.
