## Summary

Shannon's theory tells you how much a channel can carry; it leaves open the question of what it means for a signal to carry meaning at all. This paper takes that question as its starting point and derives the algebraic operations that transform syntactic information into semantic and then effective information.

The central argument is that meaning emerges at the structured boundary between order and noise, where verification becomes possible, and that this emergence requires interaction as the irreducible primitive: "to exist is to interact." From that observation, the transformation form Ax = b captures the universal computational relation between input, transformation, and output at every scale. Differentiation decomposes structure and integration accumulates it; together they generate usable meaning from raw signal, and their complementarity is what gives the framework its diagnostic power.

## Role in the Series

At the Border of Chaos identifies the boundary operation; this paper derives what it computes. The algebraic structure worked out here — particularly the Ax = b framing and the differentiation/integration pairing — is what the Zeroth Law later grounds in the unique geometry forced by two foundational observations about existence and coherence, and what the σ-calculus in Geometric Calculus then extends into a full four-rule calculus for curved space.
