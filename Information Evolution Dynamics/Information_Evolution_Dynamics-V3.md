### Conway’s Game of Life

In 1970, mathematician John Conway created a universe on a grid. Each cell is either alive (black) or dead (white), with eight neighbors. The entire grid updates simultaneously each generation according to three rules:

3.2

The First Distinction A dead cell with exactly three living neighbors becomes alive— modeling reproduction where the right density of life creates new life.

A living cell with two or three neighbors stays alive—modeling survival with adequate resources.

All other cells die— death from isolation or overcrowding.

These simple rules generate infinite complexity: patterns oscillate, emit gliders, build structures. The Game of Life is Turing complete —it can compute anything computable.

2.2

The Fundamental Constraint

Conway’s Game observes an absolute limitation: no new states ever emerge. A cell that starts as black or white can only toggle between black and white. The patterns grow arbitrarily complex through rearranging the same two states that existed from the beginning.

The limitation runs deeper than it appears. Conway’s Game can represent infinite different configurations through combinations of its two states—just as binary computers can represent any computable number or pattern through zeros and ones. The Game can encode and process any information that can be discretized into binary form.

The crucial distinction lies between representing states and generating fundamentally new states. Conway’s Game operates within a closed state space: it creates infinite combinations of black and white, yet remains unable to access anything beyond these two states. The system is ontologically closed—no computation within the Game can create “gray” or “red” or any state undefined at initialization.

Conway’s Game is a piano with only two keys. You can play infinitely complex rhythms and patterns with those two notes, even encode entire symphonies through temporal patterns. Yet you cannot generate a third frequency.

5.2

To See a World

Evolution faced the same challenge: how to extract meaningful information from the continuous electromagnetic spectrum using biological hardware that must operate through discrete detection events.

The simplest organisms began with basic photosensitive proteins that could only detect presence or absence of light, enabling them to distinguish day from night and orient toward or away from light sources. These primitive eyespots implemented our single-channel gray game in the most minimal biological hardware possible, just clusters of cells that changed chemically when struck by photons.

The first color discrimination emerged when some of these photosensitive cells developed sensitivity to specific wavelengths rather than all light equally. Most early vertebrates found the two-channel solution when S-opsins emerged in their existing vision system, creating cells that fire for short wavelengths (blue). When luminosity cells detect light but blue cells don’t, the organism perceives the longer wavelenghts (yellow/orange/red) by complementarity.

Most mammals still use this two-channel system today because it provides enough information for their survival needs without the metabolic cost of additional detector types. Dogs see the world in blues and yellows because this distinction, combined with luminosity information, suffices for navigation, hunting, and predator avoidance.

Primates developed the three-channel system through gene duplication around thirty million years ago when the L-opsin gene copied itself and one version accumulated mutations that shifted its peak sensitivity, creating two distinct variants we now call L and M opsins. This created what we now call red and green receptors from what was originally a single type, suddenly enabling discrimination within the longer wavelengths that previously all appeared as "yellow." Red fruit became visible against green foliage for the first time, providing such enormous survival advantage that this mutation swept through primate populations.

Three receptors represent the mathematical minimum for complete color encoding: luminosity plus two spectral samples at opposite ends of the spectrum would suffice (like red+blue giving you green+yellow as not-red+not-blue). But biology requires four because rods and cones operate in completely different light regimes — rods for night vision, cones

for daylight.

Evolution kept both because survival requires seeing in starlight and sunlight, not just optimal laboratory conditions.

Human vision now uses four types of photoreceptors: rods that work in dim light detecting only brightness, plus three types of cones (RGB) that work in bright light detecting different wavelength ranges. These four inputs generate six perceptual variables through opponent processing: black/white, red/green, yellow/blue.

Three cone types provide the minimum for reconstructing any spectral distribution through linear combination—the mathematical principle behind RGB color spaces in digtal screens.

Combined with rods for night vision, these four photoreceptor types generate six perceptual variables through opponent processing: black/white from luminosity, red/green from L versus M cone comparison, and blue/yellow from S cones versus the combined L+M signal.

Now this gives us a system that can represent any RGB image across time—any movie or video—and if it and can represent any possible video it can represent any visual universe within those wavelength bounds, and this is precisely what evolution converged upon through selection pressure—not specialized detectors for specific objects or patterns but an universal visual decoder, the minimum configuration capable of representing any visual information.

Natural selection eliminated everything more complex as wasteful and everything simpler as insufficient, leaving only the mathematical optimum that we can derive from first principles and that biology implements in our retinas.

Furthermore, Berlin and Kay’s linguistic studies [8] provide cultural confirmation of this mathematical and biological sequence, documenting how every human language develops color terms in almost the same order:

Tetrachromats with a fourth cone type see real colors the rest of us cannot, but these colors remain unnamed because language requires social consensus. Evolution converged on three color channels because three provided all the survivalrelevant distinctions. The fourth channel adds real information that simply doesn’t matter enough to propagate through the population or the language.
